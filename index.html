Chapter Two

The Values Sort

A card-sorting exercise that became an essay project that became a podcast. It started with a deck my therapist introduced me to.

The exercise comes from Shalom Schwartz's Theory of Basic Human Values. I've now done it with more than 200 people — in coffee shops, around kitchen tables, at retreats. The conversation it starts is always worth having.

"We say we value one thing and then spend our Tuesdays doing something else entirely. The deck just makes that visible."

— From the essay on Honesty

I wrote 66 essays — one per value, roughly 72,000 words in a single month. Now I'm reading them out loud, in order, from a teardrop camper in the yard. Season 2 will be conversations.

Chapter Three

About Nick

I've lived in McMinnville long enough to know that a place shapes you in ways that don't announce themselves — through the soil composition, the angle of winter light, the particular way people around here talk about work.

I opened Flag & Wire Coffee in 2008, which means I've made coffee for McMinnville through a recession, a pandemic, and whatever this is. The café taught me things about community and impermanence that I'm still sorting through.

I've been doing a values-based card-sorting exercise with people for years. It surfaces something true, quickly, without requiring anyone to be more vulnerable than they're ready to be. That practice is the root of everything I write.

I'm a member of a lay monastic order with my wife Kim. We have three teenagers and a five-year-old. We live on acreage with chickens, a yellow lab named Charles "Chucklet Puddin" PotPie Walton, and a teardrop camper I record podcasts in.

I follow a Jesus-flavored spiritual path outside institutional religion and have a forestry background that keeps showing up in my metaphors. I can't stop thinking about the invisible scripts that run people's lives.
